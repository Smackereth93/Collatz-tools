This tool visualizes the Collatz Conjecture using a circular mapping system. Each full circle represents the interval between successive powers of 2 or 3, with numbers' positions around the circumference determined by their proportion of the way through the interval.

This means that whereas under the operation 0.5(3n+1) numbers either map to a point further around in their current circle or a point close to the beginning of the next, they retain their position under division by 2. Thus, for large numbers, the pathway around the circle becomes very predictable, with the error from the +1 failing to make an impact on the base pattern of n*1.5^r around the circle.

Proof Ideas & Hypothesis

    
Divergence Constraint: The ergodic nature of the pathways forces them to visit the lower proportions of the circle that demand high numbers of even steps within the number's 2-adically defined pathway. A number's position in the circle puts constraints on its logarithmic pathway (definable pathway is log base 2 of the number). The bottom half of numbers require two halving steps in their LP, the bottom quarter 3 and so on. Once a pathway lands in a fraction that demands more halving steps than (3n+1)/2 steps it must start converging below its starting value. This can be visually observed extremely clearly in the circle as pathways tend to swiftly converge after they land just above a power of 2. The default number is one of the longest known pathways and achieves this by just increasing enough and avoiding numbers that demand high levels of halving enough to keep above itself for a long time, eventually the number of halving steps catch up though and it drops.

    
Loop Elimination: The accuracy of 7/12 as a rational approximation to log base 2 of 3 and Baker's bound for accuracy beyond this suggests that for large values of n, the error due to adding 1 cannot exceed the error from the irrationality of log base 2 of 3, preventing further loops.
